Credible vs. Scholarly: What's the Difference?

These terms are often confused, but they mean different things. Understanding the difference will help you choose the right sources for your assignments.

‚ö†Ô∏è Key Insight: All scholarly sources are credible, but not all credible sources are scholarly. Your professor's requirements matter!
Credible Sources Scholarly Sources
Definition Trustworthy, reliable, and accurate information from reputable sources Academic work written by experts, peer-reviewed, and published in academic venues
Authors Journalists, experts, organizations, professionals Researchers, professors, academics with credentials
Audience General public or specific professional groups Other scholars and researchers in the field
Review Process Editorial review (varies widely) Peer review by other experts
Examples New York Times, CDC, Encyclopedia Britannica, NPR Journal of Psychology, Nature, academic books from university presses

Understanding Scholarly Sources

What Makes Something "Scholarly"?

Scholarly (or "peer-reviewed" or "academic") sources go through a rigorous review process before publication:

1
Expert Authors
Written by researchers with advanced degrees and institutional affiliations (universities, research institutes)
2
Peer Review
Before publication, other experts in the field anonymously evaluate the research for accuracy, methodology, and significance
3
Citations & References
Scholarly articles cite their sources extensively, showing how the research builds on existing knowledge
4
Academic Language
Uses technical vocabulary and assumes readers have background knowledge in the field

When to Use Each Type

Use Credible Sources For:

  • Background information
  • Current events & news
  • Statistics from official sources
  • General audience writing

Use Scholarly Sources For:

  • Research papers & theses
  • Literature reviews
  • Evidence-based arguments
  • When your professor requires them!

From Topic to Search Terms

Finding good sources starts with good search terms. Here's how to transform your research question into effective searches.

Step 1: Identify Key Concepts

Break your topic into its main ideas. Don't search your entire question‚Äîpull out the important nouns and concepts.

Example Topic: "How does social media use affect depression rates among college students?"

Key Concepts:
  • Social media (what platform/technology)
  • Depression (what outcome/condition)
  • College students (what population)

Step 2: Brainstorm Synonyms & Related Terms

Different authors use different words for the same concepts. Expand your search vocabulary:

Key Concept Synonyms & Related Terms
Social media Facebook, Instagram, TikTok, social networking, online platforms, digital media
Depression Mental health, anxiety, psychological well-being, mood disorders, emotional health
College students University students, undergraduates, young adults, emerging adults, higher education
üí° Pro Tip: Use a thesaurus or look at the keywords in articles you've already found to discover more search terms!
Kay P Maye | Tulane University Libraries

Boolean Operators & Search Techniques

Boolean operators are special words that tell databases how to combine your search terms. Master these and you'll find better results faster!

The Three Magic Words

# AND - Narrows your search (both terms must appear) social media AND depression AND college students ‚Üí Results must include ALL three concepts # OR - Broadens your search (either term can appear) depression OR anxiety OR mental health ‚Üí Results can include ANY of these terms # NOT - Excludes terms (removes unwanted results) social media NOT marketing ‚Üí Removes results about social media marketing

More Powerful Techniques

"Quotation Marks"

Search exact phrases:

"social media addiction"

"college students"

Without quotes, databases search each word separately.

Truncation (*)

Find word variations:

depress* ‚Üí depression, depressed, depressive

teen* ‚Üí teen, teens, teenager, teenagers

The asterisk acts as a wildcard.

Putting It Together

# A sophisticated search combining techniques: ("social media" OR Instagram OR TikTok) AND (depress* OR anxi* OR "mental health") AND ("college students" OR undergrad*)

Library Databases: Your Research Powerhouse

Databases are searchable collections of articles, books, and other sources. Unlike Google, they're organized, high-quality, and you have full access through the library!

Types of Databases

üî¨ Subject-Specific Databases

Focus on one discipline or field. Best for in-depth research in your major.

Examples: PsycINFO (psychology), PubMed (health/medicine), JSTOR (humanities), IEEE Xplore (engineering), Business Source Complete (business)

üåê Multidisciplinary Databases

Cover many subjects. Great for interdisciplinary topics or getting started.

Examples: Academic Search Complete, Web of Science, Library Search at Tulane, ProQuest Central

üì∞ News & Current Events

Newspapers, magazines, and news archives. Good for recent events and public opinion.

Examples: Nexis Uni, Newspaper Source Plus, Nola.com (Times-Picayune), Access World News

üé¨ Streaming Media & Primary Sources

Films, music, archival documents, and historical collections.

Examples: Films on Demand, Digital Theatre+, Archives Unbound, Black Studies Center, Gerritsen Collection (Women's History)
